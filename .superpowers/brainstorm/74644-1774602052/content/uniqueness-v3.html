The Distinction Between All Three

What each product actually does and why INFER is fundamentally different

(In)Genius is essentially a super-powered research assistant. It reads documents, explores a knowledge graph, and writes reports. Very impressive. It runs multiple AI researcher agents, each with their own exploration focus, and it generated over 1,500 reports evaluated across quality dimensions using LLM-as-a-judge with 4 different models. But fundamentally, the output is a synthesis of what's already in the data. It doesn't generate new information. It organizes existing information better. The knowledge graph is static, built once from documents and then explored. Think of it as the world's best analyst reading a stack of papers and writing you a perfectly cited summary.

Sentinel is a real-time threat operations center. It aggregates over 2,200 intelligence items from 138 news sources, auto-classifies them by severity and domain, and builds a threat knowledge graph mapping relationships between events, threat actors, campaigns, and infrastructure. The graph updates continuously. New intelligence items flow in every few minutes from live RSS feeds, get classified, and get connected to existing nodes. The AI layer then synthesizes situational briefings using live web search and generates multi-phase strategic directives with specific agency assignments. The output goes from raw signal all the way to an approvable action plan. Very impressive end-to-end pipeline. But it's still fundamentally reactive. It tells you what's happening now and what to do about it. It doesn't predict what happens next.

INFER is fundamentally different because it creates emergent behavior. It takes a document, extracts entities and relationships into a knowledge graph, and then generates over a hundred autonomous AI agents, each with a unique personality, profession, backstory, biases, and social media behavior patterns. These agents don't just summarize. They debate. They disagree. They produce outcomes that weren't in the original document. A bull agent argues with a bear agent, and the result of that argument is new information that didn't exist before. That's simulation, not synthesis.

The knowledge graph also updates in real time during simulation. Every agent action, every post, every like, every repost, every argument gets converted into graph episodes through the GraphMemoryUpdater. So by the end, the graph contains not just the original document knowledge but the entire simulated social history. You can then chat with any of the agents and ask them why they said what they said, and get a response grounded in their full persona and memory of everything that happened in the simulation.

Each agent is a real character. Jerome Powell's agent responds differently than a Goldman Sachs hedge fund manager, who responds differently than a journalist at NPR. They influence each other. Opinions cascade through the simulated social network hour by hour. What you get at the end is not a summary of what was already known. It's a prediction of what people will think, say, and do when this information hits the real world.

InGenius answers "what do we know?"
Sentinel answers "what's happening and what should we do?"
INFER answers "what will people think, say, and do when this hits?"

InGenius looks backward at existing knowledge. Sentinel looks at the present. INFER looks forward.
