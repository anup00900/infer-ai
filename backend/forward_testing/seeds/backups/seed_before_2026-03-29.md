### Four Macro Themes — October 2025 to March 2026

**Theme 1 — Tariff Recovery and Its Aftermath**

April 2, 2025 — Liberation Day — sweeping reciprocal tariffs triggered a ~19% S&P 500 drawdown by April 8. The index recovered ~39% from that low to year-end as tariff rates were negotiated down. The One Big Beautiful Bill Act passed, cutting corporate and individual taxes and providing a net $127 billion consumer boost. The effective average US tariff rate settled around 16.8% — highest since the mid-1930s — but markets priced in the stable state. Heading into 2026, the Supreme Court limited certain IEEPA tariff authority and a 15% global fallback tariff replaced some rates. Apple disclosed tariffs cost it $1.1 billion in Q4 FY2025 and an estimated $1.4 billion in Q1 FY2026.

**Theme 2 — AI Capital Expenditure Supercycle: $437 Billion in 2025**

The seven largest tech companies collectively invested an estimated $437 billion in CapEx in 2025 — 61% above 2024 and ~2.5x the 2023 level. This funded AI data centers, GPU clusters, power infrastructure, and cooling systems. NVIDIA's data center revenue grew 142% in FY2025 to $115.2 billion. Data center REITs and utility companies re-rated upward. By early 2026, investor scrutiny intensified — the question shifted from "how much are you spending?" to "show us the revenue." Alphabet announced $175–185B in 2026 CapEx (stock fell 3% despite a beat). Meta guided "notably larger" 2026 CapEx than the $70–72B spent in 2025.

**Theme 3 — Iran War Energy Shock: The Largest Supply Disruption in History**

US-Israel strikes on Iran began February 28, 2026. The IEA characterised the disruption as "the greatest global energy security challenge in history" — worse than both 1970s oil shocks combined. The Strait of Hormuz — through which 20% of global oil transits — effectively closed March 4. Brent went from $73 on February 27 to $120 by March 9 (65% in 10 days). The IEA released a record 400 million barrels on March 11, described as buying ~20 days. As of March 25: Brent at $100–104. US gasoline at $3.94/gallon. Goldman projects $110 avg March–April. Oxford Economics $140/bbl scenario implies recession-level drag on global growth if sustained 2 months. Q1 2026 earnings consensus (set before the war) called for +11.7% EPS growth — now under pressure. No Q1 2026 earnings have been reported as of today.

**Theme 4 — Fed Chair Succession and Monetary Policy Pause**

The Fed cut rates three times in 2025 (Sep, Nov, Dec) for a total of 75bps, bringing the rate to 3.50–3.75%. Both January and March 2026 FOMC meetings held rates unchanged. Jerome Powell's term expires May 2026. Names circulating: Kevin Warsh (perceived less dovish), Christopher Waller. Any signal of reduced Fed independence could spike Treasury yields and compress equity multiples. The 10-year Treasury yield moved from 3.90% in October 2025 to 4.27% on March 19 — rising even as the Fed held short-term rates. Market-implied pricing suggests only ~1 rate cut for all of 2026 — a dramatic shift from multi-cut expectations at year-end 2025.

## Section 4 — Global Context: USA-Israel War on Iran and Market Impact

### The War — What Happened and Where Things Stand

US and Israel began coordinated strikes on Iran on February 28, 2026. Supreme Leader Khamenei was killed on Day 1 along with dozens of senior IRGC commanders. Iran retaliated immediately with missile and drone attacks on Israel and US military bases across the Gulf. As of March 25 — Day 26 — the war is active with no ceasefire. ### Strait of Hormuz — Why It Matters to Every Portfolio

20% of global seaborne oil supply transits the Strait of Hormuz daily — approximately 20 million barrels per day. Since March 4 this has been reduced to a trickle. The consequences cascade across every asset class:

- Kuwait, Iraq, Saudi Arabia, and UAE collectively cut oil production by at least 10 million barrels per day by March 12 — storage is full with nowhere to ship.
- QatarEnergy declared Force Majeure on all LNG exports from Ras Laffan — the world's largest LNG liquefaction facility, responsible for 20% of global LNG production. Asian LNG spot prices doubled to $25.40/MMBtu on March 4.
- 21 commercial vessels have been attacked since March 1. UK Maritime Trade Operations authority threat level: CRITICAL.
- US gasoline prices up 23% in three weeks. CPI was 2.4% in January — the oil shock threatens to add 0.5–1.2 percentage points in Q1–Q2 2026.
- The IEA's 400 million barrel strategic reserve release is described by analysts as buying approximately 20 days of coverage. After that, prices spike again unless Hormuz reopens.

### Global Spillovers

**Asia:** China, India, Japan, and South Korea account for 75% of Hormuz oil exports and 59% of LNG exports. China declared Force Majeure on certain oil contracts. India facing energy emergency. South Korea announced it would cap pump prices for the first time in nearly 30 years. LNG spot prices in Asia more than doubled.

**Europe:** EU exploring fuel subsidies and electricity price caps. France: TotalEnergies capped gas and diesel prices until end of month. European Commission President von der Leyen said the Commission is "exploring subsidies or capping the gas price." European diesel +20% since war began.

**GCC food emergency:** Gulf Cooperation Council states rely on the Strait for over 80% of their caloric intake. By mid-March, 70% of the region's food imports were disrupted.

**Fertilizer chain:** Brazil — responsible for 60% of global soybean exports — is nearly entirely dependent on imported fertilizers, with nearly half of its supply transiting the Strait of Hormuz. A sustained fertilizer shortage could trigger a drop in global crop yields.

**Global hedge funds:** Suffered worst losses since Liberation Day (April 2025) during the peak oil shock week of March 8–9. Multiple funds reported margin calls and forced position unwinds — amplifying the S&P 500 decline from the ATH.
